## Introduction

An operational amplifier is a high-gain differential device used in many analog applications such as amplification, filtering, and signal processing.

The voltage follower is one of the simplest op-amp configurations. In this circuit, the output voltage directly tracks the input voltage. The purpose is not to increase amplitude but to provide isolation between stages.

It ensures:
- Very high input impedance  
- Very low output impedance  

This helps avoid loading and allows proper signal transfer.

---


## Theory

A voltage follower is derived from the non-inverting amplifier configuration.

The gain of a non-inverting amplifier is:

Av = 1 + (Rf / Rin)

For a voltage follower:
Rf = 0  
Rin = ∞  

So,

Av = 1 + (0 / ∞)  
Av = 1  

Hence,

Vout = Vin  

The output is directly connected to the inverting input, resulting in complete negative feedback.

---

## Characteristics

### High Input Impedance
- Almost zero input current  
- Source is not loaded  

### Low Output Impedance
- Can supply current to load  

### Unity Gain
- Output equals input  

### Negative Feedback
- Improves stability and accuracy  

---

## Working

- Input is applied to the non-inverting terminal  
- Output is connected back to the inverting terminal  

Because of very high gain:

V+ ≈ V−  

Thus,

Vin ≈ Vout  

The op-amp keeps adjusting the output so that both inputs remain nearly equal.

---

## Frequency Response

- At low frequency → gain = 1 (0 dB)  
- At high frequency → gain reduces  

Bandwidth is high compared to other configurations.

The voltage follower operates as expected. It provides unity gain, no phase shift, and accurate signal reproduction. It is suitable for buffering and impedance matching applications.
